## What it claims

- Customers’ understanding of what counts as a loyalty-building activity differs across cultural contexts.
- The meaning of a “loyalty program” should not be assumed from the provider/researcher perspective alone.
- Loyalty-program components can be grouped into joining/identification, mechanism, and benefit.
- Benefits are more important than joining mechanisms or program mechanics in shaping whether customers perceive an activity as loyalty-building.
- Intercultural differences affect how loyalty-program components are interpreted.
- In the study, Polish, Serbian, and Kuwaiti respondents differed in how they interpreted belonging, recognition, discounts, material rewards, and personalized communication.
- Collect-and-redeem mechanisms may produce behavioural lock-in without necessarily producing affective loyalty.

## What evidence it provides

The paper uses a conjoint design to study how customers in Poland, Serbia, and Kuwait interpret loyalty-program components.

The study defines loyalty-program attributes as:

- **Joining/identification**
  - registration
  - card

- **Mechanism**
  - collecting
  - belonging

- **Benefit**
  - recognition
  - material reward
  - lower/discounted price
  - personalized communication

The authors use a fractional factorial design with eight combinations of attributes and levels, framed as marketing scenarios for a fictitious coffee shop. The sample includes 364 respondents:

- Poland: 148
- Kuwait: 113
- Serbia: 103

The analysis uses conjoint analysis, OLS part-worth utilities, ANOVA/MANOVA, and post-hoc tests.

Key reported findings include:

- The **benefit** attribute is the most important loyalty-program component across all three countries.
- Belonging is perceived as a loyalty mechanism more by Kuwaiti respondents than by Polish and Serbian respondents.
- Recognition is more important for Kuwaiti respondents than for Polish and Serbian respondents.
- Personalized communication is more important for Serbian and Kuwaiti respondents than for Polish respondents.
- Material reward matters more for Polish respondents than for Serbian and Kuwaiti respondents.
- Discounts matter more for Serbian and Polish respondents than for Kuwaiti respondents.
- Country effects are generally more important than gender effects.

The evidence is about consumer interpretation of loyalty-program components, not cybersecurity or automated web abuse.

## What it claims

- Arkose positions itself as a proactive fraud deterrence platform for protecting account integrity and digital experiences from fraud, bot attacks, phishing, scraping, account takeovers, and agentic AI abuse.
- Arkose Titan is framed as a unified platform for account fraud, bot attacks, and AI-agent policy enforcement.
- Arkose Bot Manager claims to detect, disrupt, and defeat advanced bot and human-driven attacks without hurting legitimate user flow.
- Arkose Bot Manager claims to use 225+ risk signals and the Arkose Global Intelligence Network to detect evasive threats.
- Arkose emphasizes adaptive/dynamic challenges that evolve in real time to counter emerging attack vectors.
- Arkose’s public solution taxonomy covers account takeover, credential stuffing, loyalty point theft, API security, human fraud farms, fake account creation, bonus abuse, SMS toll fraud, MFA compromise/reverse-proxy phishing, scraping protection, and agentic AI.
- ACTIR is presented as Arkose’s threat-intelligence unit focused on threat hunting, risk intelligence, disarmament, and virtual enforcement.
- ACTIR material links Arkose to disruption of Storm-1152, a cybercrime-as-a-service group associated with large-scale fake-account creation.
- Arkose’s Quarterly Threat Actor Patterns landing page claims malicious traffic surged nearly 20% in Q2 2025 as AI-powered attack automation democratizes cybercrime.
- Arkose’s 2026 Agentic AI Security Report landing page claims 97% of enterprise leaders expect a material AI-agent-driven security or fraud incident within 12 months, while only 6% of security budgets are allocated to AI-agent risk.
- Axios coverage of an Arkose survey reports that only 20% of surveyed companies felt very prepared for high-volume AI-powered bot attacks, 56% said generative AI increased cyber-threat frequency and sophistication, and 88% reported increased AI-powered bot attacks over the previous two years.
- These are vendor and survey/report claims, not independent detection-performance evidence.

## What evidence it provides

The evidence is mostly product pages, report landing pages, and press/news coverage of Arkose survey findings.

### Arkose Bot Manager

The Bot Manager page is useful for understanding Arkose’s product framing. It says Bot Manager detects threats that slip through conventional defences, including:

- account takeovers
- SMS toll fraud
- fake account creation
- other advanced attacks

The page names several product concepts:

- 225+ risk signals
- Arkose Global Intelligence Network
- dynamic challenges that evolve in real time
- differentiation between good users and malicious bots
- dashboards and actionable intelligence

The distinctive Arkose angle is not just detection but cost-imposition: dynamic challenges and friction are used to make automation and abuse less economically viable.

### ACTIR and Storm-1152

Arkose’s ACTIR page describes ACTIR as a counterintelligence unit focused on:

- threat hunting
- risk intelligence
- disarmament
- virtual enforcement
- bot incursions
- fraud farms
- hybrid automated attacks

The page links ACTIR to the Storm-1152 disruption and describes Storm-1152 material as “gateway services to cybercrime.” It also links to related materials describing a group that built and sold tools to bypass enterprise security controls and create hundreds of millions of phony online accounts.

This is valuable as threat-intelligence/vendor evidence for cybercrime-as-a-service, fake-account factories, and hybrid bot/human fraud operations. It is not a reproducible academic dataset.

### Quarterly Threat Actor Patterns, Q3 2025

The public landing page says malicious traffic surged nearly 20% in Q2 2025 as AI-powered attack automation democratized cybercrime.

The full report appears gated from the landing page, so this entry should not treat detailed internal report findings as known unless the report PDF is separately obtained.

### 2026 Agentic AI Security Report

The public landing page says the report was produced by Arkose Labs with TechStudio and draws on a global survey of 300 enterprise leaders across North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific.

The landing page highlights five dimensions:

- readiness
- detection
- attribution
- insider risk
- AI-agent security/fraud response

It claims:

- 97% of enterprise leaders expect a material AI-agent-driven security or fraud incident within 12 months
- only 6% of security budgets are allocated to AI-agent risk
- only one in four enterprises can prove AI-agent involvement in an incident
- credentialed AI agents redefine insider-risk models

The page is relevant to the modern AI-agent section, but it is a survey/report landing page, not direct technical evidence of attacks or detections.

### Axios coverage of Arkose AI-bot survey

Axios reports that Arkose surveyed 188 US-based cybersecurity professionals from September 3–23, each at manager level or above. Reported findings include:

- only 20% of companies felt very well prepared to defend against high-volume AI-powered bot attacks
- 56% said generative AI increased cyber-threat frequency
- 56% said generative AI made cyber threats more sophisticated
- 88% said their companies faced increased AI-powered bot attacks in the prior two years
- examples included using leaked identities to create retail accounts for sign-up bonuses and using bots to log into loyalty accounts and cash out points as gift cards

This is useful because it gives more survey-method detail than the Arkose landing pages, but it remains survey evidence and vendor-associated.

## Framing distance

- **What real-world bot/abuse problem does this source approximate?**  
  Arkose material is strongest for account-integrity abuse: fake accounts, credential stuffing, loyalty theft, SMS toll fraud, phishing-linked account compromise, and cybercrime-as-a-service. It is also useful for the project’s attacker-cost framing because Arkose’s product story emphasizes dynamic challenges and making fraud economically unviable.

- **What does it fail to represent?**  
  It does not provide independent detection accuracy, false-positive rates, raw traffic data, adversarial test design, or reproducible technical methodology. Several report pages are landing pages for gated reports, so only the public claims can be used unless the full PDFs are obtained. The 2026 Agentic AI report appears survey-based, so it measures perceived risk/readiness as much as observed abuse. The Axios-covered survey uses 188 US cybersecurity professionals, which is useful but not a global measurement of bot prevalence.

- **What additional evidence would be needed to go further?**  
  Full report PDFs; independent customer-side evaluations; details of survey sampling and questionnaires; detection-performance data; adversarial tests with Playwright, Puppeteer, Selenium, anti-detect browsers, residential/mobile proxies, CAPTCHA solvers, AI agents, and human fraud farms; comparison with Cloudflare, DataDome, HUMAN, Kasada, Netacea, Akamai, Imperva, and open academic baselines; evidence on whether dynamic challenges measurably raise attacker cost.

## What it cannot show

- It cannot prove Arkose Bot Manager’s detection accuracy.
- It cannot verify product claims about 225+ signals or adaptive challenge effectiveness.
- It cannot establish global prevalence of AI-powered bot attacks.
- It cannot prove AI-agent incidents will occur at the rates suggested by survey respondents.
- It cannot show how often dynamic challenges defeat advanced bots in production.
- It cannot independently quantify false positives or user-experience impact.
- It cannot replace academic/empirical sources such as Iliou et al., Acien et al., FP-Inconsistent, FP-Inspector, or JA4/TLS studies.
